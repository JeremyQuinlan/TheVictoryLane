MARKET OUTLOOK

Bears took this week decisively, but the bull case isn't dead — it's just bruised. Q2 earnings outside of mega-cap tech are genuinely strong, particularly financials and industrials, and the data center build-out shows zero signs of deceleration (GEV, INTC, and SMCI all confirmed that, as did OpenAI's $750B cloud spending plan per the WSJ). The real risk is the hyperscaler balance sheet problem: capex is swamping operating cash flow, stock and bond prices are under pressure, and capital markets are being asked to fill the gap at exactly the wrong time. Add in oil, tariffs, and AI-driven inflation, and the path of least resistance remains choppy. A negotiated Iran détente is still the base case — Trump has neither the appetite nor the logistics for a ground war — but until that materializes, energy markets stay vulnerable and so does sentiment.

---

GEOPOLITICAL

- The Middle East situation deteriorated sharply this week. Iran rejected a heavily favorable MOU, its hardliners remain at war with its own negotiating team, and U.S. rhetoric is escalating without a clear military endgame. Trump is caught between unwillingness to commit ground troops and inability to accept the status quo indefinitely.

- A second front has opened, with Houthis targeting Saudi vessels in and around the Red Sea, compounding pressure on global energy supply routes alongside the ongoing Hormuz threat.

- Energy markets are acutely exposed: Ukrainian strikes have gutted Russian refining infrastructure, global inventories have been aggressively drawn down in recent months, and now both major Gulf chokepoints are under assault simultaneously.

COMPANY NEWS

GOOGL disappointed with higher capex guidance and vague language around restoring Gemini to frontier model leadership. Free cash flow was negative in Q2, and management described third-party compute usage as merely a "bridging strategy" — implying Google plans to exit external neocloud contracts once its own infrastructure scales, raising real capacity-glut concerns for that segment.

TSLA was the bigger negative surprise — very weak margins, cautious commentary from Musk on Optimus timelines, and negative free cash flow in the quarter.

INTC, SMCI, and GEV all delivered solid results and reinforced that the data center buildout remains firmly intact. SAP, NOW, and ROP were fine in legacy software — no worse than feared in what remains one of the most unloved groups in the market.

AMD raised eyebrows with a $5B investment into Anthropic while simultaneously exploring providing financial backing for data center leases — adding to concerns about circular financing arrangements proliferating across the AI ecosystem.

STM was hit hard after Q3 guidance came in light of expectations.

PEGA warned that "unprecedented" AI-driven changes are causing CTOs to delay legacy software purchases — echoing what IBM flagged the prior week and dealing a fresh blow to the software group.

CALX (Calix) posted a large gross margin miss and warned of further pressure from surging memory prices — a dynamic likely to surface across Q2 more broadly.

Albertsons posted a significant earnings miss and slashed guidance, reinforcing the pain in consumer staples, already among the market's most unloved sectors. Nestlé also disappointed.

Comcast noted on its call that theme park attendance across the Orlando market began softening in June and that the trend has carried into Q3 — more than management anticipated.

PHM (PulteGroup) offered a cautious but slightly encouraging read on housing: "early signs of stabilization in select geographies," even as affordability, rate volatility, and macro uncertainty continue to weigh.
