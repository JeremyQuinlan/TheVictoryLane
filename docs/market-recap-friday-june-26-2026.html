RATES & FED

May PCE came in cooler than feared on a month-over-month basis, with the headline rise missing consensus — a meaningful data point suggesting May likely marked the peak of the recent inflation bulge on a year-over-year basis. The sharp drop in oil should layer in significant disinflationary pressure through the second half. If that trajectory holds, a rate cut by year-end or early 2027 is a live scenario.

---

MARKET OUTLOOK

Bears won the week despite a strong fundamental hand dealt to the bulls — blow-out Micron earnings, bullish Qualcomm F29 guidance, and a softer PCE print couldn't offset the accelerating deterioration in AI sentiment. The core concern is structural: hyperscalers and frontier model firms are burning cash faster than they generate it, making the entire capex cycle dependent on capital markets and circular financing — not a durable foundation. As AI's stranglehold on the investment narrative loosens, equal-weight S&P outperformance over the cap-weighted index is the logical expression of that rotation, and it's already showing up. The 1990s analog everyone loves is misleading — shrinking deficits, a peace dividend, and deflationary globalization defined that era; today, all three are running in reverse.

---

GEOPOLITICAL

- The US-Iran détente remains broadly on track, with Hormuz shipping insurance rates falling and reports suggesting Israel may exit part of its Lebanese buffer zone — collectively pulling oil prices meaningfully off recent highs (WSJ).

- That said, Iran attacked a cargo ship in the Strait this week, testing the framework of the deal, and separately Iran is reportedly eyeing a $40 billion windfall from fees tied to Hormuz reopening — underscoring how messy the path to a permanent settlement will be (WSJ).

- Large gaps between US and Iranian positions on the nuclear program persist, and Lebanon remains a live risk variable.

- Russia, losing ground in Ukraine, is under mounting internal pressure — reportedly requesting gasoline from Kazakhstan to offset domestic shortages caused by Ukrainian strikes. A cornered Moscow is an unpredictable one.

COMPANY NEWS

MU (Micron) delivered blow-out FQ3 results and superb FQ4 guidance, but the real signal was incremental color on strategic customer agreements (SCAs), which suggest the current EPS boom has legs — as EPS confidence rises, multiple expansion should follow. Bears pushed back, flagging rising memory capex (including upcoming IPOs from China's CXMT and SK Hynix's US ADR listing), softening PC/smartphone unit demand, and declining average DRAM content in servers.

QCOM (Qualcomm) provided bullish F29 guidance at its analyst meeting and unveiled new chip architectures specifically designed to reduce memory dependency — a direct shot across the bow of the HBM complex.

ON (ON Semiconductor) agreed to acquire SYNA (Synaptics) for approximately $7 billion in a deal that kept M&A activity healthy heading into summer.

TECH (Bio-Techne) was acquired by Merck KGaA for approximately $11.3 billion, sparking a broad rally in lab equipment and services names.

ICLR (ICON plc) reported healthy earnings with particularly robust B2B metrics, further lifting the lab/services sector.

CBRS reported earnings where guidance failed to meet elevated expectations; management spent notable time on the call emphasizing its chips avoid HBM — a defensive posture that didn't go unnoticed. Shares slumped sharply post-IPO.

CCL (Carnival) trimmed guidance citing fallout from the Middle East conflict.

DRI (Darden) saw Olive Garden comps come in slightly light, and F27 EPS guidance missed Street estimates.

FDX (FedEx) reported operating margins below expectations for the quarter.

JEF (Jefferies) missed on EPS due to asset management headwinds and a higher compensation ratio.

WGO (Winnebago) missed on both EPS and revenue and cut full-year guidance.
