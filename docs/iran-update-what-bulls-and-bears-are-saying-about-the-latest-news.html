MARKET OUTLOOK

The Iran situation remains the dominant risk variable for markets right now, and the complacency baked into energy and risk assets looks increasingly fragile. Hormuz disruptions aren't hypothetical — ships are actively being attacked, and Tehran is serious about imposing transit fees. That said, Trump's deep reluctance to fully re-escalate is a real floor under sentiment, so expect volatility without a full breakdown.

GEOPOLITICAL

- Iran/US strikes: The Pentagon conducted airstrikes against Iranian targets Tuesday night — described as the most significant military action since the MOU was signed — with the White House explicitly framing it as a stern message to Tehran (WSJ, Axios). The US still considers the ceasefire technically in effect, and a US official told Reuters that Washington continues working "in good faith" toward a final agreement.

- Hormuz tension: The two sides hold starkly different interpretations of the MOU, particularly around Hormuz. Ships not coordinating with Tehran have been coming under attack for several weeks, and Iran appears serious about imposing transit fees. One proposed resolution: mirror the voluntary-payment framework used in the Malacca Strait (WSJ).

- Nuclear talks stalled: "There has been no substantive progress made yet" on a final nuclear agreement, per WSJ. Lebanon also remains unresolved, adding another layer of complexity to any durable détente.

- Iranian oil sanctions: US Treasury on Tuesday revoked a license authorizing the sale of Iranian oil — a meaningful tightening move even as diplomatic talks nominally continue (Reuters).

- Bottom line: The bull case rests on neither side wanting full resumption of hostilities, OPEC+ supply expansion, and eventual Hormuz normalization. The bear case is that the market is pricing a clean détente that simply doesn't exist on the ground. The honest read: this is messy, non-linear, and the latest airstrike was no love tap — but full re-escalation remains the tail, not the base case.
