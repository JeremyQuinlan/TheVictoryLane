RATES & FED

Yields are stubbornly refusing to move lower despite falling oil and a weak June jobs report — a clear signal that markets remain skeptical on the inflation trajectory and are pricing in fiscal risk. The FOMC minutes were a two-sided affair: dovish in tone on the rate path (many officials see rates ending the year "within or slightly below" current levels), but hawkish on balance sheet policy, with the QT task force leadership leaning firmly against an enlarged Fed balance sheet. NY Fed consumer survey showed 1-year inflation expectations up 20 basis points to 3.7% — the highest since September 2023.

MARKET OUTLOOK

Bears edged this week on a combination of Iran escalation and Samsung's earnings stumble, which together crystallized a key risk heading into Q2 season: numbers may be fine, but expectations are dangerously elevated. Tech bifurcation is now structural — infrastructure names (MU, NVDA, LITE) and software/services names (CRM, NOW, MSFT) are trading as two mutually exclusive universes, and that divergence is likely to widen. Apple's quiet resurgence near the top of the market-cap rankings is the market sending a message: investors are starting to treat the AI arms race as a MAD cycle, and the one large-cap sitting it out is looking increasingly attractive. The approaching Q2 season should still be strong in aggregate, but the Samsung read-through is a warning shot — beats that fall short of whispers will be punished hard.

GEOPOLITICAL

- Iran fired on ships in the Strait of Hormuz, prompting the US to respond with its most intense airstrike campaign in weeks; Trump declared at the NATO summit that the ceasefire is over — though a US official said Thursday that Washington remains committed to a negotiated resolution (NYT). Qatar is now mediating between Washington and Tehran to revive the peace process (NYT).

- The MOU between the US and Iran remains vague on core issues including Hormuz control and Lebanon; the original JCPOA took years to finalize, and this process may be no different given deep divisions within Iranian leadership.

- Ukraine is turning the tide against Russia, and Trump has greenlit Kyiv manufacturing Patriot interceptors and conducting deep strikes into Russian territory. Putin, more cornered than at any prior point in his tenure, carries escalation risk — including nuclear saber-rattling.

- Russia banned diesel exports this week amid domestic shortages driven by Ukrainian strikes on energy infrastructure (Reuters).

- Trump threatened Spain with economic retaliation at NATO, with US officials already drafting a potential embargo list of Spanish goods — a modest but potentially EU-relationship-defining development (WSJ).

COMPANY NEWS

NVDA surged ~3.6% Wednesday after *The Information* reported China will permit select domestic AI firms to purchase a limited number of H200 chips — a meaningful demand signal for the near term.

META was the bull story of the week: new frontier models launched Thursday alongside an API that marks the company's first attempt to charge external customers for AI access. The API is priced aggressively, effectively using Meta's advertising cash flow as a weapon against closed-model rivals like OpenAI and Anthropic. The volume of positive press was compared to Google's Gemini 3 reception last year.

AAPL has quietly staged an impressive rebound after late-June margin anxiety, helped by reports it will use Chinese DRAM for devices sold in China. Apple is now within striking distance of reclaiming the largest market cap in the world from Nvidia — a signal that investors are rewarding its AI sideline positioning.

DAL reported a beat and guided ahead of plan for Q3 and full-year 2026 — a clean, confidence-inspiring print to kick off travel sector earnings.

LEVI delivered a beat-and-raise, adding to the constructive early-season tone.

PEP opened Q2 season on a soft note Thursday — EPS roughly in line with consensus and full-year guidance maintained, but US beverages and food both missed, and management flagged incremental caution on the domestic consumer (no change to annual guidance).

COST underperformed Thursday after June comparable sales came in slightly below expectations.

AMZN raised $25 billion in debt markets this week with little friction, underscoring that capital markets remain open to hyperscaler paper — for now.
