RATES & FED

The Friday BLS jobs report — a deeply disappointing +57K on the Establishment Survey with even weaker Household Survey figures — is expected to spark a sharp Treasury rally. The market is likely to reprice from 1–2 Fed hikes this year toward 0–1, with a bias toward zero. That said, the Fed is unlikely to pivot dovish outright, as slowing job creation is being offset by labor force contraction, which actually pushed the unemployment rate *lower*.

---

MARKET OUTLOOK

The macro backdrop is constructive — oil is tumbling, Eurozone CPI printed cooler than expected, and Fed rhetoric at Sintra (Warsh, Lagarde) was largely benign — but none of that matters much right now because US equities are being driven almost entirely by shifting tech dynamics. The rotation story is the one to watch: money that crowded into pick-and-shovel AI names for several quarters is starting to rotate toward hyperscalers and legacy software, and that squeeze has room to run. Chip stocks look vulnerable — the "buy the dip" consensus is too relaxed, which historically means more pain ahead. Legacy software is arguably the most mispriced corner of the market, with high-quality names trading at distressed valuations despite a future that, while uncertain, is being discounted far too pessimistically.

---

GEOPOLITICAL

- Oil has fallen sharply as Hormuz traffic rebounds and supply expands via higher OPEC+ quotas, the UAE's departure from OPEC, Iraq threatening to exit the cartel, full removal of Iranian oil sanctions, and rising US and Venezuela output — a meaningful lid on inflation and yield pressure.

- US-Iran détente remains the base case, but the reality is messier than markets are pricing: the two sides still differ on MOU interpretation, kinetic exchanges continue weekly, Hormuz fee demands from Iran and Oman remain unresolved, and Lebanon is still a live risk (Bloomberg, FT).

- The White House formally walked away from USMCA, opening a likely contentious renegotiation period, while simultaneously working to restore the average import tax rate to pre-SCOTUS-decision levels — tariff uncertainty is back on the table (WSJ).

- The SCOTUS ruling in the Fed/Lisa Cook case was a win for monetary independence.

- Washington lifted restrictions on Anthropic's Mythos and Fable models, easing fears of draconian frontier AI regulation.

COMPANY NEWS

AVAV (AeroVironment) surged after FQ4 results crushed expectations: EPS of $1.84 vs. Street $1.41, EBITDA of $140MM vs. $126.1MM, and sales of $641.6MM vs. $556.4MM. Bookings were strong with a book-to-bill of 1.4x and product gross margins rebounded sharply quarter-over-quarter. The one blemish was FY27 EBITDA guidance of $315MM vs. the Street's $346MM.

GIS (General Mills) spiked Wednesday on strong FQ4 EPS upside driven by robust margins, FY27 guidance roughly in line with consensus, and a sizable new cost-cutting program.

META shares surged Thursday after Bloomberg reported the company plans to sell excess compute capacity to third-party customers — a move that accelerates capex monetization but simultaneously raised bear-case fears that the data center industry has overbuilt.

NKE (Nike) finished Wednesday higher after holding its prior EPS forecast intact via better margins, though management trimmed revenue guidance and offered a gloomy macro demand update, noting negative demand linearity during the quarter.

MSM (MSC Industrial) posted strong FQ3 EPS of $1.43 vs. Street $1.27, with operating margins up 160 basis points year-over-year to 10.6%, beating consensus by roughly 60 basis points. Sales of $1.047B edged past the $1.032B estimate, and FQ4 guidance was bullish.

STZ (Constellation Brands) delivered solid FQ1 upside, benefiting from resilient category demand, price increases, healthy execution, and market share gains.

PRGS (Progress Software) surged 16% Wednesday on a clean beat-and-raise quarter.

CNXC (Concentrix) fell 11% Tuesday after cutting full-year guidance — a reminder that the market has zero tolerance for negative surprises in legacy software/services, regardless of valuation.

AAPL (Apple) is reportedly in talks to source memory from China's CXMT and YMT to ease margin pressure, and Nikkei reported the company is preparing an aggressive iPhone launch cycle into 2027, aiming to exploit component market turmoil to gain share.

Telecom names T, TMUS, and VZ sold off on growing concern that SpaceX's Starlink is positioning as a serious competitor in consumer mobile.
