RATES & FED

The Fed delivered a hawkish curveball Wednesday under new Chair Warsh — markets repriced H2 hike expectations from roughly 20 basis points to roughly 40 basis points in a single session. What rattled markets more than the guidance shift was the radical change in institutional posture: the statement and press conference were unusually brief and opaque, and Warsh has stood up five task forces to conduct a wholesale reevaluation of US monetary policy. Globally, the hawkish tide is rising fast — the BOJ hiked June 16, the ECB hiked June 11, and Indonesia, Norway, the Philippines, the RBA, and Taiwan all moved hawkish within the past two weeks.

---

MARKET OUTLOOK

Bears edge the week. The Iran MOU removes the single biggest headline overhang, but it was widely anticipated — Trump had telegraphed his reluctance to escalate militarily for weeks, and the S&P was already materially higher before the ceasefire. The real new risk is the Warsh Fed, which has injected a fresh and arguably more durable source of uncertainty into markets. Meanwhile, trade is not off the table — Trump flirted with terminating USMCA Wednesday, and the White House is actively working to push average tariff rates back to pre-SCOTUS levels by end of summer. AI remains the dominant market force by an order of magnitude, but the cracks are widening: enterprise IT departments are pushing back on token costs, regulatory pressure on frontier models is intensifying after the White House forced Anthropic to pull its latest release, and free cash flow across the sector is deteriorating even as EPS looks strong (WSJ, Thursday).

---

GEOPOLITICAL

- The US and Iran reached a Memorandum of Understanding, ending active conflict. The MOU is non-binding and vague, kicking off a 60-day negotiation window. Oil supply could ultimately exceed pre-war levels given immediate sanctions removal, higher OPEC+ quotas, the UAE's exit from OPEC+, and rising non-OPEC production — but normalcy in the Strait of Hormuz is still months away at minimum.

- Trump raised the prospect of terminating USMCA on Wednesday, and his administration is pursuing a return to pre-SCOTUS tariff levels by late summer, keeping trade risk alive despite Iran resolution.

- The White House directed Anthropic to pull its most powerful new models from the market entirely, raising serious questions about the regulatory ceiling for frontier AI development.

COMPANY NEWS

ACN (Accenture) reported Thursday morning with a sharp bookings shortfall that validated the bear thesis on AI displacing legacy IT services firms. The release also created collateral anxiety for software names, given how much IT services revenue is tied to implementing legacy SaaS.

ADBE (Adobe) — flagged again this week as another illustration of why tech investors can't afford to ignore AI: legacy software firms are encountering real fundamental headwinds, forcing investors to chase the theme regardless of valuation discomfort.

JBL (Jabil) stated Wednesday that demand for AI infrastructure remains "extremely strong," echoing the relentlessly bullish demand commentary from the hyperscaler complex.

CRWV (CoreWeave) executives told Bloomberg's Tae Kim that AI demand appears to "intensify every day," adding to the capex-as-religion narrative.

META is reportedly exploring raising tens of billions in incremental capital for AI infrastructure, while simultaneously considering abandoning frontier model leadership and simply leasing out its data centers (FT).

AAPL (Apple) — Tim Cook told the WSJ Thursday that Apple is lobbying Washington for waivers allowing US OEMs to source memory from Chinese firms, and would consider writing a large check to fund expanded memory capacity. If successful, this could materially pressure MU (Micron), Samsung, and SK Hynix.

KR (Kroger) posted a modest EPS miss Thursday, with gross margins down 33 basis points year-over-year, partially driven by deliberate price investments (discounting).

FOX shares fell sharply after investors reacted negatively to its bid to acquire ROKU.

FI (Fiserv) dropped Monday following the unexpected departure of its CEO.

BMW (BMWYY) pre-announced a severe margin cut — EBIT margins in the auto segment now guided to 1–3% versus prior guidance of 4–6%, citing China weakness and ongoing Middle East supply chain disruption.
