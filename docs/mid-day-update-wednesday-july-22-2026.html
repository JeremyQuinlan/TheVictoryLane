RATES & FED

Treasuries are under pressure with yields rising 1–3 basis points across the curve. Fed expectations have shifted notably hawkish, with markets now pricing in roughly 42 basis points worth of hikes. The spike in Brent toward $94 is a key driver of the inflation-risk repricing.

---

MARKET OUTLOOK

Markets are effectively treading water ahead of a monster earnings slate after the close — GOOGL, TSLA, IBM, NOW, and TXN among them — and until those prints land, direction is anyone's guess. The pattern so far in Q2 is instructive: results have been "fine," but investors already expected robust performance, so the bar is brutally high (GEV and TEL both got punished today despite solid underlying numbers). The geopolitical premium in energy is real and rising, which complicates the Fed's calculus. On Iran, the most likely resolution remains a negotiated détente rather than full military escalation — Trump has shown little appetite for boots-on-the-ground — but the path there is messy and volatile. Stay selective; AI infrastructure and energy have the wind at their backs.

GEOPOLITICAL

- Secretary Rubio said Iran "is not serious about talks," dampening hopes for near-term diplomacy, while Iran's interior minister visited Pakistan with no sign of de-escalation (Bloomberg, CNBC).

- Iran has demonstrated its ballistic missile capabilities are more formidable than widely assumed (WSJ), and Houthi forces have repositioned missiles and drones near the Bab el-Mandeb strait, ready to attack shipping and impose a maritime blockade around Saudi Arabia (Bloomberg).

- The White House approved a landmark civilian nuclear deal with Saudi Arabia that could open the door to domestic uranium enrichment — a significant regional realignment (WSJ).

- Trump announced 100% tariffs on generic drugs effective 8/1/28, rising to 200% on 8/1/29; branded/patented drug policy is unchanged (Bloomberg).

- Trump appointed Adam Candeub, a known big-tech critic, as DOJ antitrust chief (FT).

- BOJ officials are reportedly open to a faster rate-hike pace as yen weakness generates upside inflation risks (Bloomberg).

- UK June CPI: headline cooled to +2.6% (vs. +2.7% expected), but core (+2.6% vs. +2.5%) and services (+3.6% vs. +3.5%) ran hot (Reuters).

COMPANY NEWS

SMCI (Supermicro) preannounced FQ4 revenue near the low end of $11–12.5B guidance, but gross margins are tracking ~16% — roughly double consensus — and orders during the quarter were a staggering >$60B (book-to-bill ~5.5x), lifting the entire server complex including DELL and HPE.

AMD signed a 2GW AI chip supply deal with Anthropic and is investing $5B into the company, with talks underway to backstop Anthropic data center leases (WSJ).

AAPL is planning a sweeping upgrade cycle across its Mac lineup — MacBook Air, iMac, MacBook Pro, Mac mini, and Mac Studio — to capture surging AI demand (Bloomberg).

GEV (GE Vernova) Q2 EBITDA missed ($1.25B vs. $1.29B expected), though revenue beat ($11.1B vs. $10.8B), free cash flow was enormous ($5.1B vs. $1.3B expected), orders surged +88% organic to $24.2B, and full-year guidance was raised for both sales and cash flow. Stock sold off on elevated expectations.

CME beat Q2 estimates on sales ($1.7B vs. $1.67B) and EPS ($2.99 vs. $2.90).

T (AT&T) posted Q2 EBITDA of $12.3B (vs. $12.1B expected) and free cash flow of $4.7B (vs. $4.4B), with wireless postpaid net adds of +432K (vs. +325K) and fiber adds of +367K (vs. +303K). Full-year guidance reiterated with accelerated buybacks flagged.

MCO (Moody's) Q2 EPS of $4.68 beat the $4.28 Street estimate; revenue came in at $2.185B vs. $2.086B expected, MIS revenue spiked 25%, and operating margins expanded 440 basis points to 55.3%. Full-year EPS guide nudged to $16.50–$17, buyback raised to $3B.

WAB (Wabtec) Q2 EPS of $2.76 beat $2.61; sales rose 17.5% to $3.18B vs. $3.07B, margins expanded 80 basis points to 21.9%, and full-year revenue and EPS midpoints were raised ~$110M and ~30 cents respectively.

PHM (PulteGroup) Q2 EPS of $2.48 beat $2.36; housing gross margins of 25% topped the 24.3% estimate, orders rose 6% to 7,536, and management cited tentative signs of market stabilization.

PM (Philip Morris) Q2 EPS of $2.20 beat $2.04; smoke-free organic revenue rose 11.8%, though investors were disappointed the FXN EPS guide ($8.11–$8.26) was merely reiterated rather than raised.

COF (Capital One) Q2 EPS of $5.81 beat $4.68, driven largely by provisions coming in ~$1B below expectations at ~$3B; NII/NIM fell slightly short.

CB (Chubb) Q2 EPS of $7.26 beat $6.75 on a favorable combined ratio and investment income, but net premiums written disappointed as management pulled back from unfavorably priced business.

PEGA (Pegasystems) FQ2 EPS of 35 cents missed the 44-cent estimate as "unprecedented AI market changes caused clients to delay purchasing decisions" — echoing IBM's recent warning on legacy software demand and dragging the broader software complex.

CALM (Cal-Maine Foods) FQ4 EPS of -76 cents badly missed the +8-cent estimate; sales of $552M fell short of $595M as egg prices cratered.

TEL (TE Connectivity) FQ3 EPS of $2.94 beat $2.84 on higher-than-expected sales of $5.16B vs. $5B, but the stock declined — a symptom of lofty expectations across the industrial/tech complex.

OTIS Q2 EPS was inline at $1.01, but operating margins fell 180 basis points to 15.2% vs. the 16% consensus, and full-year EPS/cash flow guidance was cut to $4.01–$4.05 from $4.20–$4.24.
