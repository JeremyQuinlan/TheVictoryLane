MARKET OUTLOOK

Wednesday is shaping up as probably the most consequential single day of this earnings season, with GOOGL, TSLA, IBM, NOW, and TXN all reporting after the close alongside GEV and TEL before the open. The early tell is not encouraging for tech bulls: VAT Group posted blow-out orders (+40% Q/Q, +102% Y/Y) and got sold anyway, continuing a pattern where exceptional chip-related results simply can't clear an expectations bar that has been set at the stratosphere. SMCI's pre-market surge on a ~5.5x book-to-bill is the counterpoint — infrastructure demand is clearly not slowing — but the distinction between picks-and-shovels winners and software/platform names getting disrupted by AI is widening fast. With Brent surging toward $94.40 on Middle East deterioration and energy markets at risk of a genuine retightening, the macro backdrop adds another layer of complexity that equities have been too comfortable ignoring.

GEOPOLITICAL

- Secretary Rubio stated flatly that Iran "is not serious about talks," with no ceasefire pathway visible (CNBC). Iran's interior minister visited Pakistan but the meeting produced zero sign of de-escalation (Bloomberg).

- The Houthis have repositioned missiles and drones and are actively prepared to strike commercial shipping near the Bab el-Mandeb strait, raising the prospect of the Red Sea becoming a new front in the conflict (Bloomberg). Some Saudi tankers have already reversed course after receiving Houthi threats.

- Iran is demonstrating that its ballistic missile capabilities are significantly more formidable than Western analysts assumed — the US can intercept most but not all incoming fire, and even a modest success rate is sufficient to damage critical regional infrastructure (WSJ).

- The White House has approved a landmark nuclear agreement with Saudi Arabia providing a civilian nuclear program and potentially opening the door to uranium enrichment (WSJ).

- Tariff update: markets shrugged at Tuesday's news as the White House is simply restoring average import tax rates to pre-SCOTUS-decision levels — expected, telegraphed for months (FT, WSJ, CNBC). Separately, Trump announced generic drugs face a 100% tariff effective 8/1/28 and 200% as of 8/1/29; branded/patented drugs are unaffected (Bloomberg). Sandoz said it would engage with the White House on the plan (WSJ).

- BOJ officials are openly discussing a faster-than-anticipated tightening pace as yen weakness stokes upside inflation risk (Bloomberg). UK June CPI: headline +2.6% (vs. Street +2.7%), but core came in at +2.6% (vs. +2.5% expected) and services at +3.6% (vs. +3.5%) — a mixed print that keeps the BOE uncomfortable (Reuters).

- Trump appointed Adam Candeub, a known big-tech skeptic, as DOJ antitrust chief (FT). Crypto legislation faces new headwinds as progressives attack Sen. Gillibrand's brokering role on the Clarity Act compromise (Axios).

COMPANY NEWS

SMCI is surging ~15% pre-market after pre-announcing FQ4/June results: revenue near the low-end of the $11–12.5B guidance range, but gross margins coming in around 16% — roughly double consensus — and orders exceeding $60B, implying a book-to-bill of approximately 5.5x. The infrastructure buildout is not slowing.

PEGA delivered another AI-era warning, missing FQ2 EPS (35c vs. 44c expected) with ACV and revenue both below plan, citing "unprecedented changes in the AI market" causing clients to delay purchasing decisions — an echo of what IBM flagged last week.

RDDT is reportedly reconsidering its content-licensing relationship with Google, part of a broader publisher revolt against Google's AI-driven search changes that keep users on Google's own pages rather than clicking through (WSJ).

CB (Chubb) beat Q2 EPS ($7.26 vs. $6.75 expected), driven by a favorable combined ratio — nearly 200 basis points better than anticipated — though net premiums written disappointed as management deliberately passed on soft-priced property business.

COF (Capital One) posted Q2 EPS of $5.81 vs. $4.68 expected, with provisions coming in ~$1B below forecast at ~$3B as the primary driver; NII/NIM fell slightly short.

ALK (Alaska Air) reported Q2 EPS of -92c vs. guidance of -$1.00 — better than feared but still deep in the red, with Q3 EPS guidance mid-point of 50c far below the Street's $1.48.

IBKR (Interactive Brokers) beat Q2 EPS/revenue (69c/$1.88B vs. 63c/$1.78B), with margin loans spiking 67% to $108.5B driving the NII outperformance.

AIR (AAR Corp.) posted solid FQ4 results: EPS $1.53 and revenue $928MM vs. Street estimates of $1.38/$895MM, with bullish guidance.

GT (Goodyear) will permanently close its Fayetteville, NC plant, cutting 1,750 jobs and incurring ~$200MM in cash charges, but expecting ~$90MM in Americas operating income improvement in 2027 and ~$270MM annually from 2028 onward.

WFRD (Weatherford) beat Q2 sales/EBITDA ($1.105B/$223MM vs. $1.07B/$210MM) with free cash flow of $139MM crushing the $85.5MM estimate, though full-year EBITDA guidance was trimmed slightly to $951MM–$1.046B.

NKE (Nike) will tighten wholesale distribution in China to defend brand positioning and drive full-price sales (Reuters).

AMG (Affiliated Managers) faced selling pressure Tuesday after the US Treasury flagged concerns over investment products designed to deliberately generate capital losses (Bloomberg).

SK Hynix denied speculation it is planning to acquire Intel's Ohio fab (Digitimes).
