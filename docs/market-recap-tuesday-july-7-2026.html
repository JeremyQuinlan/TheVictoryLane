RATES & FED

Treasuries sold off Tuesday with yields rising roughly 6–7 basis points, driven by the spike in oil following Iran's Hormuz attacks. The market is now pricing approximately 35 basis points of Fed hikes in H2, up from ~30 basis points Monday. NY Fed's Williams told Fox he's modestly more hopeful on inflation given the recent oil pullback, but the NY Fed's June consumer survey showed 1-year inflation expectations rising 20 basis points to 3.7% — the highest since September 2023 (NY Fed).

MARKET OUTLOOK

Tuesday was largely a rotation story — money fleeing AI pick-and-shovel names landed in hyperscalers, software/services, insurance, REITs, and utilities, which is fine on the surface but masks real stress underneath. The core bear case is that tech expectations heading into earnings are dangerously elevated compared to Q1, and Samsung's "good but not good enough" reaction proved the market will punish anything short of a blowout. The SemiAnalysis report flagging Nvidia as the de facto "central bank of AI" — providing revenue guarantees to neoclouds to keep data center construction going — raises serious circular-financing red flags that the market isn't pricing yet. Add surging memory costs, open-source Chinese model competition eroding frontier pricing power, and a $25B Amazon bond deal that attracted only half the demand of its March offering (Bloomberg), and the setup into earnings season looks fragile despite solid underlying fundamentals.

GEOPOLITICAL

- Iran fired ballistic missiles at two commercial ships near the Strait of Hormuz early Tuesday, striking a Qatari LNG vessel; the US subsequently revoked a general license authorizing Iranian oil sales, with a US official warning of "consequences" while noting negotiations continue (WSJ, Bloomberg, Reuters).

- Beijing is reportedly weighing restrictions on overseas access to China's top AI models — a direct threat to the international market share gains DeepSeek and others have been accumulating (Reuters).

- Trump revived calls for US control of Greenland Tuesday, risking a fresh crisis with Europe (FT).

- Belarus's Lukashenko stated he will not join Russia's war in Ukraine, removing one significant escalation risk (FT).

- China is accelerating oil purchases from Middle Eastern producers, raising concerns about inventory restocking that could put a floor under Brent prices (FT).

- Kuehne + Nagel pushes back on the idea that land trucking can substitute for Hormuz — the strait remains structurally critical to global shipping (FT).

COMPANY NEWS

NVDA (Nvidia): A SemiAnalysis report details how AI debt financing could exceed $7T by 2029 — second only to mortgages in ABS — with Nvidia providing revenue guarantees to neoclouds to backstop construction. Separately, a Bloomberg survey shows Chinese firms are actively shifting away from Nvidia toward domestic silicon.

META (Meta): Launched Muse Spark Image, its first new image generation model since overhauling its AI strategy (Bloomberg, FT). Separately, four US states are seeking $1.4 trillion in penalties in a youth safety trial accusing the company of deliberately designing addictive social media products (Reuters).

MSFT (Microsoft): Replacing OpenAI and Anthropic models with its own proprietary "MAI Models" in select products to cut costs (Bloomberg).

RIVN (Rivian): Announced a 75 million share secondary offering; guided Q2 revenue to ~$1.6B, comfortably above the Street at ~$1.44B.

VRTX / CRNX (Vertex / Crinetics): Vertex acquiring Crinetics for $85/share cash (~$10B equity value, $8.5B enterprise value) — a ~100% premium to Monday's close.

EFX (Equifax): Acquiring Círculo de Crédito, Mexico's fastest-growing credit bureau, for $750M enterprise value.

ON (ON Semiconductor): Divesting two manufacturing facilities; expects ~$35M in annualized cost savings, phasing in from 2027 with full impact in 2028.

FISV (Fiserv): A consortium of major banks — JPM, BAC, WFC, PNC — held preliminary talks about acquiring a payments network owned by Fiserv; most analysts see fierce regulatory risk making a deal unlikely (WSJ).

SPCX (SpaceX): Wall Street initiating coverage with bullish ratings ahead of Nasdaq-100 inclusion, though the stock's recent slump raises questions about appetite for the broader neocloud financing wave (Bloomberg, Reuters).

Samsung: Preliminary Q2 results beat print consensus — sales KRW 171T vs. Street KRW 169.2T, operating income KRW 89.4T vs. Street KRW 84.2T — but fell short of elevated whisper numbers, sparking a selloff in chip/pick-and-shovel names.

AMZN (Amazon): Priced a $25B bond offering that drew $62B in demand — solid headline, but roughly half the oversubscription seen in its March deal, adding pressure to tech-linked credit (Bloomberg).
