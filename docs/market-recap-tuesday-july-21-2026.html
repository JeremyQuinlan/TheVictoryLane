RATES & FED

Treasuries sold off Tuesday with yields rising 1–5 basis points across the curve. Fed expectations shifted hawkish, with markets now pricing approximately 39 basis points of hikes in H2. The ECB holds Thursday with no change expected, though another hike is being penciled in for the September 10 meeting.

MARKET OUTLOOK

Tuesday's rally was tech-led and feels justified given what's coming out of the earnings pipeline — the AI capex story remains intact, and Wednesday's reports from GOOGL, TSLA, NOW, and TXN will either confirm or complicate that narrative. The equal-weight S&P only ticked up 16 basis points, which is a subtle reminder that breadth isn't quite as robust as the headline numbers suggest. Energy markets are at genuine risk of retightening — Middle East escalation, degraded Russian production capacity from Ukrainian strikes, and depleted global inventories (especially in China) create a combustible setup. Jamie Dimon's warning that markets are underpricing geopolitical and fiscal risk (CNBC) deserves serious attention, even if markets shrugged Tuesday. The path of least resistance is higher short-term, but the risk premium embedded in oil and rates is not fully reflected in equities.

GEOPOLITICAL

- The US-Iran conflict is approaching a critical inflection: either a negotiated 10-day ceasefire (being pushed by Pakistan and Qatar) or a significant military escalation — potentially including strikes on Pickaxe Mountain, where Israel believes Iran has relocated covert nuclear centrifuges (Axios, WSJ). US intelligence assessments suggest current airstrikes are unlikely to alter Iranian behavior (Washington Post).

- Pentagon budget pressures are mounting rapidly, with munition shortages and escalating risk to American personnel constraining options — Trump has no appetite for boots on the ground, making a negotiated exit the only realistic off-ramp (Washington Post).

- Strait of Hormuz traffic has slumped since the US reimposed its blockade on Iran (CNBC). Houthi rebels are now targeting Saudi tankers, with some reportedly reversing course after receiving threats — opening a new front in regional risk.

- The White House is preparing to restore average tariff rates to pre-SCOTUS decision levels, framing it as maintenance rather than escalation (FT, CNBC). A 50% tariff on Canadian hockey sticks and wine has been announced separately.

- Washington is weighing restrictions on American companies' access to Chinese open-source AI models, which would benefit closed-model players like OpenAI and Anthropic but keep token costs elevated (Axios, WSJ). China is separately considering controls on AI exports including model weights and chips (FT).

- China launched a coordinated market support campaign following a tech-driven selloff, with record inflows into tech ETFs, while vowing to meet its full-year growth target despite a Q2 shortfall (Bloomberg).

COMPANY NEWS

GM posted Q2 EPS of $3.57 vs. the Street's $3.19 on revenue of $48B vs. $46.6B expected. Full-year EPS guidance midpoint rises to $13 from $12.50, driven by EV cost reductions, growing software/services revenue, and favorable vehicle mix enabling steady pricing.

MMM delivered Q2 EPS of $2.40 vs. $2.25 expected, with 5.4% organic growth (Street at 3.2%) and op. margins of 24.9%. Full-year EPS guidance raised to $8.80–$8.95 from $8.50–$8.70.

HAS beat Q2 EPS at $1.28 vs. $1.13, with sales of $1.13B vs. $1.066B expected across both physical toys and gaming. Full-year guidance raised.

DHR beat Q2 EPS ($1.94 vs. $1.84) and revenue ($6.26B vs. $6.1B), but cut full-year sales growth to +3–4% from +3–6% on soft respiratory testing. Diagnostics op. margins fell 160 basis points year-over-year.

DHI beat FQ3 EPS ($3.20 vs. $2.98) but trimmed full-year revenue guidance to $32.5–$33B from $33.5–$34.5B on light orders and ongoing housing headwinds.

EFX reported Q2 EPS of $2.25 vs. $2.20 but guided Q3 EPS to $2.20 at the midpoint vs. the Street's $2.25. Full-year range tightened to $8.39–$8.69.

MSCI missed Q2 on both EPS ($4.94 vs. $4.99) and revenue ($867MM vs. $870MM), with full-year expense guidance moving higher.

CCK posted Q2 EPS of $2.49, up 16% and well above the Street's $2.16. Full-year guidance raised to $8.30–$8.50 from $7.90–$8.30 on solid can demand.

NOC beat Q2 EPS at $7.68 vs. $6.83, aided by a 6.3% tax rate, though segment op. margins fell 120 basis points. Full-year sales and EPS guidance nudged higher.

SYF delivered Q2 EPS of $2.59 vs. $2.12, with the beat largely from lower provisions. Full-year EPS guide raised to $9.25–$9.50 from $9.10–$9.50.

SCHW beat Q2 EPS ($1.62 vs. $1.55) and revenue ($7.072B vs. $6.91B) with $120B in core net new assets and 1.4M new brokerage accounts opened.

CALX suffered a significant gross margin miss driven by spiking memory costs — a dynamic likely to recur throughout Q2 earnings season.

NVDA made positive remarks on the ramp of its Vera Rubin architecture toward customers (The Information, Bloomberg).

ORCL saw credit default swap costs hit near 18-year highs amid balance sheet concerns and massive capex commitments; the company may also be required to post significant collateral for a Wisconsin data center project (Bloomberg, FT).

TSMC is set to raise chipmaking prices by up to 10% in 2027, with advanced nodes potentially seeing steeper increases (Nikkei).

UTZ agreed to be acquired by Intersnack for $14.25/share in cash — a roughly 90% premium to Monday's close.

IEP will sell Pep Boys to Mavis Tire Express for approximately $700MM in cash (WSJ).

KHC struck a supply and licensing deal with Disney to serve Kraft products at North American Disney properties and use Disney characters on packaging (WSJ).

KLAR/AAPL — Apple will partner with Klarna to launch a new device leasing program called "Apple Upgrade" (Bloomberg).

MSFT announced an expanded partnership with Mistral, integrating Mistral's models across Azure, Copilot Studio, and Microsoft
