MARKET OUTLOOK

Tariffs have faded as the dominant market anxiety — geopolitics and hyperscaler capex are running hotter as concerns right now — but that complacency could be tested if trade policy continues to escalate in arbitrary ways. This Canada move looks worse in headline form than in practice, given the sweeping list of exemptions (energy, potash, steel, aluminum, autos, critical materials all carved out), but the pattern of impetuous, symbolic actions is itself a slow-burn risk. The 30-day runway to Aug 20 keeps the negotiating door technically open, and the market will likely treat this as noise unless Canada retaliates in kind. Still, every episode like this chips away at business confidence and adds cost friction that eventually lands on the consumer.

GEOPOLITICAL

- Trump announced late Monday (~5pm ET) plans to impose a 50% tariff on select Canadian goods under Section 338, citing "continued discrimination against and unreasonable and unequal treatment of U.S. commerce" — with the White House specifically calling out Canadian policies on autos, alcoholic beverages, and cheese. Tariffs take effect in 30 days (Aug 20). (White House fact sheet)

- Critically, these Section 338 tariffs apply regardless of USMCA status, effectively signaling that the trade deal is functionally dead as a protective mechanism. Major carve-outs include energy, potash, fish, critical materials, and goods already covered by Section 232. (Vital Knowledge / White House)

- The White House's choice to name "wine and hockey sticks" as emblematic targets is telling — this reads as largely symbolic, possibly motivated as much by Canadian wildfire smoke tensions as by genuine trade grievance. That said, the broader signal is one of continued unpredictability in U.S. trade policy, which creates real cost burdens and corporate uncertainty regardless of intent.
