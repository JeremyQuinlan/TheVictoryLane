RATES & FED

Fed Governor Waller opened the week hawkish, warning that another hot core inflation print could force the FOMC to tighten "in the near term" — but Tuesday's CPI came in cooler than expected and took the edge off that threat. NY Fed President Williams struck a more dovish tone Wednesday, saying inflation has "peaked and should edge down in coming quarters" and that policy is currently well positioned. Logan and Schmid also delivered hawkish remarks on upside inflation risks, while Fed Chair Warsh reiterated his zero-tolerance stance on persistently elevated inflation.

---

MARKET OUTLOOK

Bears won the week in tech, but it's critical to separate signal from noise: this selloff was driven more by crowded positioning, stale sentiment, and a narrative shift than by a sudden deterioration in fundamentals. That said, the IBM warning, TSM's margin disappointment, and Moonshot AI's open-source model launch Friday were genuine catalysts — and the AI trade's dependence on capital markets to fund capex means that a sustained equity slump could become a self-fulfilling slowdown in data center construction. The broader market has a real cushion in strong Q2 bank earnings and cooler inflation, but if AI-linked stocks don't stage a rapid recovery, the macro spillover risk is significant. Watch the debt and equity issuance pipeline closely — indigestion there is the clearest leading indicator of stress.

GEOPOLITICAL

- The US-Iran conflict remains stalemated — ongoing US airstrikes prompted Iranian retaliation all week, and while Trump clearly wants out (the MOU was skewed heavily in Iran's favor), there's no viable military path to resolution. JD Vance confirmed on the Rogan podcast that the White House has no appetite for an Iraq-style forever war or turning Iran into a failed state. A negotiated détente remains the most likely outcome, but the risk of escalation spiraling out of control is real.

- Tehran reportedly ordered the Houthis to close the Red Sea if the US strikes Iranian power infrastructure (Reuters). Separately, Saudi Arabia-Yemen tensions are rising, putting both Hormuz and Red Sea shipping lanes at elevated risk simultaneously.

- Russia is now targeting Ukrainian ships in the Black Sea, threatening global grain markets on top of existing energy infrastructure damage from Ukraine's sustained counterstrikes on Russian facilities.

- Brazil faces new 25% US tariffs, though several major categories are exempt. Tariffs have taken a backseat to geopolitics but remain a live risk given the chaotic rollout pace.

- South Korea's central bank hiked rates as expected but warned of further tightening ahead, and unveiled new curbs on leveraged single-stock ETFs.

COMPANY NEWS

IBM issued a negative preannouncement — the bombshell of the week — raising two uncomfortable questions: are CTOs front-running AI-driven hardware inflation by gutting software budgets, and is double/triple ordering creating phantom demand signals across the chip supply chain?

ASML and TSM (TSMC) both reported solid numbers with encouraging qualitative commentary, but investors punished TSM on slightly soft margin guidance, non-AI end market weakness, and another large capex increase. The read-through: chip capacity is being added at an accelerating pace, which is a red flag if/when the cycle turns.

NFLX (Netflix) disappointed — soft Q3 guidance and a pullback in engagement disclosure granularity spooked investors.

HCA cut full-year guidance and, alongside ISRG, cited headwinds from declining ACA enrollment after premium subsidies expired.

ELV raised full-year guidance by less than its Q2 beat warranted — the market read that as a miss in all but name.

UNH and ABT were standout beats. JBHT, MAN, PLD, TRV also delivered upside. Bank earnings were broadly excellent — trading, capital markets, advisory, and wealth management revenues all surged, with much of that tied directly to AI-related activity.

Ericsson warned that AI-driven component price spikes are pressuring margins — a problem facing tech OEMs broadly.

PNR also issued a negative guidance update, adding to the HCA/IBM trio casting a shadow over the broader reporting season.
