MARKET OUTLOOK

The earnings tape tonight is sending mixed signals — AI infrastructure is booming (SMCI bookings of $60B+) while legacy software is quietly cracking under the weight of deferred decisions. The PEGA miss echoes IBM's recent caution and puts ServiceNow squarely in the spotlight Wednesday night. Consumer-facing names like ALK remain in a tough spot with cost pressures and a Q3 guide that falls well short of Street expectations. Until the AI spending deferral narrative either gets confirmed or refuted by NOW, the market is likely to stay cautious on enterprise software broadly. Watch financials as a relative bright spot — provisioning tailwinds and improving efficiency ratios are giving names like COF room to run.

GEOPOLITICAL

- Weatherford's management explicitly flagged "significant disruption in the Middle East due to the Iran conflict," though the company still delivered strong free cash flow of $139MM in Q2 against a Street estimate of $85.5MM (company press release).

COMPANY NEWS

ALK (Alaska Airlines) posted Q2 EPS of -$0.92, better than its own guidance of -$1.00, but fuel headwinds and weather issues more than offset solid operational performance and RASM gains. The real problem is the Q3 guide: EPS midpoint of $0.50 versus Street consensus of $1.48 — a massive shortfall that the sanguine language in the press release doesn't come close to papering over.

CB (Chubb) delivered strong Q2 EPS of $7.26 versus the Street's $6.75, with the beat driven by a combined ratio that came in nearly 200 basis points better than anticipated and slightly stronger investment income. That said, the core combined ratio only outperformed by about 35 basis points, and net premiums written fell short as management is consciously walking away from underpriced property business — particularly large account and E&S — citing "overly soft underwriting conditions." They're paying a growth penalty now but expect it to dissipate.

COF (Capital One) put up Q2 EPS of $5.81 versus the Street's $4.68, a big beat driven largely by provisions coming in roughly $1B lighter than expected at ~$3B, with core efficiency also running about 60 basis points better than anticipated. NII/NIM was a slight miss, but the overall print is solid.

IBKR (Interactive Brokers) reported Q2 EPS/revenue of $0.69/$1.88B versus the Street's $0.63/$1.78B, with better NII/NIM as the primary driver, aided by a 67% spike in margin loans to $108.5B. Commission revenue grew 30% but was largely in line with expectations.

AIR (AAR Corp.) beat on FQ4/May results with EPS/revenue of $1.53/$928MM versus the Street's $1.38/$895MM, and forward guidance is described as bullish.

GT (Goodyear) is permanently closing its Fayetteville, North Carolina plant, resulting in 1,750 job cuts and approximately $200MM in cash charges. The move is expected to improve Americas segment operating income by ~$90MM in 2027 and ~$270MM annually from 2028 onward.

WFRD (Weatherford) posted modest Q2 upside with sales/EBITDA of $1.105B/$223MM versus the Street's $1.07B/$210MM. Free cash flow was the standout at $139MM versus the $85.5MM estimate, even amid Middle East disruption tied to the Iran conflict.

PEGA (Pegasystems) missed Q2 EPS at $0.35 versus the Street's $0.44, with ACV and revenue both slightly below plan. Management cited "unprecedented changes in the AI market" causing clients to delay purchasing decisions — language that directly echoes IBM's recent commentary and raises broader concerns about legacy enterprise software spending heading into the ServiceNow print Wednesday night.

SMCI (Supermicro) guided FQ4/June revenue to the low end of its $11–12.5B range, but gross margins are projected at ~16% at the midpoint — roughly double consensus. More importantly, FQ4 orders surpassed $60B, implying a book-to-bill of approximately 5.5x. Investors will rightly focus on margins and bookings over the minor revenue miss.
